Colosseum

The Roman Colosseum is considered a wonder of the world due to its architectural innovation, including its unique oval shape that allowed better views for the audience. As one of the few mostly intact structures from the Roman Empire, it stands as a monument to ancient Rome’s architectural and engineering prowess. It symbolizes Roman dominion and ingenuity, and is a major source of tourism revenue for Italy. The Colosseum was once the greatest amphitheater in the world, hosting gladiatorial fights and naval battles.

You can see a section of the arena that was once underground

Emperor Domitian added this underground section, called the hypogeum. Gladiators and wild animals stayed in this bi-level, underground section of the arena before fights. They entered through the tunnels and emerged in front of the crowd thanks to an intricate rope-and-pulley system controlling 80 vertical passageways.

Naumachia, mock sea battles

These were reenactments on water of historical naval battles with condemned criminals as sailors. The first naumachia was given by Julius Caesar in 46 BC to celebrate his victories in Egypt. Caesar created a basin near the Tiber River on the Campus Martius and flooded it. Sixteen life-size ships manned by 4,000 rowers and 2,000 slaves reenacted a battle between the Egyptians and the Tyrians. It was a fight to the death for men and rowers.
